Strong coverage
- Survival foundations: priorities, risk assessment, household routine discipline.
- Water: collection, purification, storage, rationing.
- Fire / cooking / heat: fuel discipline, low-fuel cooking.
- Shelter: improvised shelter, insulation, storm repair.
- Medicine / first aid / sanitation: bleeding, wound care, fracture handling, diarrhea/rehydration, sanitation systems, medicine storage caution.
- Psychology / social dynamics: panic control, leadership, conflict reduction.
- Movement / navigation: evacuation logic, movement with vulnerable people.
- Communication / information resilience: paper plans, records, rumor control.
- Tools / repairs / outages: maintenance, battery and lighting discipline.
- Food production / agriculture: staple crop planning.
- Disasters: floods, storms, wildfire, heat, cold, smoke.
- Historical / wartime lessons: sieges, shortages, wartime civilian continuity.
- Dystopian / AI / surveillance resilience: digital dependency, automation failure fallback.
- Rebuilding / preservation: household organization, archive design.

Moderate coverage
- Pharmacology: strong on caution, storage, scarcity logic; still not a medication reference compendium.
- Anatomy / biology: strong on survival-useful physiology; still needs deeper body-system expansion and disease pattern coverage.
- Community rebuilding: present but still expandable into governance, apprenticeship, workshop systems, and education frameworks.

Weak or incomplete areas
- Edible and poisonous plants by region
- Fishing and trapping basics
- Detailed anatomy by organ system
- Detailed biology of infection, immunity, healing stages
- More disaster-specific response matrices (earthquake, drought, blackout city survival, prolonged smoke events)
- Detailed menstrual hygiene, infant feeding under hardship, elder chronic disease continuity
- Broader agricultural cycles, compost systems, irrigation methods, seed saving by crop
- Expanded community medicine and field clinic organization
- Offline reference appendices such as measurements, symptom indexes, and quick triage tables

Recommended expansion principle
Add depth where it increases practical utility, not just file count. Prefer:
1. more detailed reference entries,
2. broader but non-duplicative coverage,
3. stronger cross-linking between medical, sanitation, and logistics topics,
4. more structured rebuilding knowledge.
